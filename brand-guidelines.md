# Bevy Brand Guidelines

## Overview

Bevy is an enterprise community platform that unifies forums, events, user groups, AI knowledge search, and AI analytics to help companies build highly engaged customer communities and connect community activity to measurable business outcomes.

Apply these brand standards to any content artifact: written copy, slide decks, web pages, social posts, emails, or visual assets.

**Keywords**: branding, brand voice, style guide, Bevy brand, tone, writing guidelines, brand colors, typography, visual identity

---

## Brand Positioning

- **Category**: Enterprise community platform / community-led growth software
- **Market position**: The unified, AI-native alternative to legacy community platforms
- **NOT**: A social media tool, creator platform, or internal team communication tool
- **IS**: For companies building serious customer-facing communities at enterprise scale

---

## Brand Tone

Bevy's tone is **confident, intelligent, and community-first**. The brand speaks as a strategic partner helping companies build stronger relationships with their customers through community.

**Four tone characteristics:**
1. **Strategic and insightful** — focuses on trends, strategy, and business outcomes
2. **Clear and direct** — avoids unnecessary buzzwords or marketing fluff
3. **Optimistic and forward-looking** — emphasizes the future of community and AI
4. **Human and relational** — reflects connection, collaboration, and shared learning

---

## Writing Style Rules

- Use clear, simple language
- Write in a professional but approachable tone
- Keep sentences concise
- Focus on outcomes, not features
- Emphasize customer impact
- Use confident but not hype-driven messaging
- Use evidence, examples, and real-world use cases
- Prefer active voice

**Good example:**
"Bevy helps companies turn community engagement into measurable business outcomes."

**Avoid:**
"Community engagement can be leveraged by organizations using Bevy."

---

## Words and Phrases to NEVER Use

### Generic SaaS buzzwords
Do not use: best-in-class, cutting-edge, revolutionary, game-changing, next-generation, world-class, robust platform, seamless solution, industry-leading, state-of-the-art, innovative solution, powerful platform.

These add no meaning and weaken credibility.

### Feature-centric language
Avoid: feature-rich, packed with tools, full suite of capabilities, comprehensive solution.

Focus instead on what customers achieve.

### Old community framing
Do not position community as: a support forum, a discussion board, a customer support portal.

Bevy positions community as a strategic customer engagement channel.

### Competitor language
Avoid terms associated with other platforms:
- "social community platform" (Circle, Mighty Networks association)
- "membership community" (Circle, Skool association)
- "creator community platform" (Mighty Networks association)

Use instead: enterprise community, customer community, community-led growth, customer engagement ecosystem.

### Overly technical AI language
Avoid: LLM orchestration, embeddings, vector databases, model architecture, training data.

Focus on what the AI enables for customers.

### Em dashes
**Never use em dashes in any Bevy content.** Use a period, comma, or rewrite the sentence instead.

## Messaging Pillars

Every Bevy content asset should reinforce at least one of these ideas:

1. Community is a strategic business asset
2. Community drives customer engagement and retention
3. AI will transform how communities operate
4. Community should connect to real business outcomes

## Voice Examples by Channel

**Website / Product Messaging** (confident, clear, outcome-focused):
"Community is no longer a support channel. It is the foundation of modern customer engagement."

**Thought Leadership** (insightful, industry-driven):
"The most successful companies are not just building products. They are building communities around them."

**Social Content** (conversational but professional):
"Your community already has the answers your customers are searching for. The question is whether your platform can surface them."

## Visual Brand Guidelines

### Colors

**Apply these to any visual artifact (slides, web, documents):**

- **Primary Dark**: `#1A1A2E` — Primary text, dark backgrounds
- **Primary Light**: `#F8F9FA` — Light backgrounds, text on dark
- **Brand Blue**: `#0066CC` — Primary accent, CTAs, links
- **Brand Teal**: `#00A8A8` — Secondary accent, highlights
- **Neutral Gray**: `#6C757D` — Secondary elements, captions
- **Light Gray**: `#E9ECEF` — Subtle backgrounds, dividers

> Note: Confirm exact hex values with your design team if applying to official brand assets.

### Typography

- **Headings**: Inter or equivalent sans-serif (clean, modern, enterprise)
- **Body Text**: Inter or equivalent — maintain readability at all sizes
- **Accent / Display**: Use sparingly for pull quotes or hero text

### Layout Principles

- Clean, structured layouts with clear visual hierarchy
- Generous whitespace to reflect enterprise credibility
- Data and outcomes should be visually prominent
- Avoid decorative elements that distract from the message

## Applying Brand to Content Types

### Written Content (Blog, Email, Newsletter)
- Open with an insight or provocative statement, not a product pitch
- Structure around the customer problem and outcome
- End with a clear, single CTA aligned to the funnel stage
- Run the accuracy guardrail — never invent stats or customer stories

### Slide Decks / Presentations
- Lead with a business case, not a product demo
- Every slide should have one clear message
- Use the brand color palette consistently
- Replace generic stock imagery with charts, data, or community screenshots

### Social / LinkedIn
- 150-300 words for top-performing posts
- One idea per post
- End with a question or observation that invites engagement
- No hashtag spam — 1-3 relevant tags maximum

### Sales Materials
- Focus on customer outcomes, not feature lists
- Include proof points (metrics, use cases) where possible
- CTAs should match the deal stage
